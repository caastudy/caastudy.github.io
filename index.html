About this Study

Shaken by severe compromises, the Webâ€™s Public Key Infrastructure (PKI) has seen the addition of several security mechanisms over recent years. One such mechanism is the Certification Authority Authorization (CAA) DNS record, that gives domain holders control over which Certification Authorities (CA) may issue digital certificates for their domain. After standardization as RFC 6844 and adoption by the CA/B forum, checking of this record became mandatory for member CAs on Sep. 8, 2017.
The success of CAA hinges on the behavior of three actors: CAs, domain name holders and DNS operators. We empirically study their behavior, and observe that CAs exhibit patchy adherence in issuance experiments, domain name holders configure CAA records in encouraging but error-prone ways, and only 4 of the large DNS operators enable customers to configure CAA records. Furthermore, using historic CAA data, we uncover anomalies in already issued certificates. We disseminated our results in the community. This has already led to specific improvements at several CAs and revocation of mis-issued certificates. Furthermore, in this work, we suggest ways to improve the security impact of CAA. To foster further improvements and to practice reproducible research, we share raw data and analysis tools.

CAA Adoption

Note: We have removed some invalid data points, for example in early December. The removed data points were typically caused by network problems.
Note 2018-02-21: We observed high jitter between Feb 6 and Feb 20, 2018. This has been caused by rate limits hit due to the explosive growth in Feb 6th. We addressed the issue and the scans seems table again since 2018-02-20.

We perform regular scans of >200M domains for CAA support and show their CAA adoption above.
Anomalies: The peak in "TUM-DETAIL with CAA or CNAME2CAA" in early November stems from a hoster enabling CAA on its base domain, to which about 15k customer domains point.
The jump in early February also stems from 2 hosters enabling CAA on its base domain, with 60k and 57k base domains pointing to it via CNAME.
Starting February 9, 2018, we are also removing domain names of the pattern google[a-f0-9]{16}.sld.publicsuffix from our scan. These are used to assert domain DNS control by google by placing such as CNAME that points to google.com (see here). As google.com deploys CAA, these base domains were counted as deploying CAA, which they are effectively not.
