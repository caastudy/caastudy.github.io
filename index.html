About this Study

Shaken by severe compromise, the Web PKI has seen the addition of several security mechanism over the past years. One such mechanism is the Certificate Authority Authorization (CAA) DNS records type. The CAA record allows DNS domain name holders to control the set of Certificate Authorities (CA) that are allowed to issue digital certificates for a domain. After standardization as RFC 6844 and adoption by the CA/B forum, adherence to this record has become mandatory for the CA/B member CAs on September 8, 2017.

The success of CAA will hinge on three actors, which we investigate in this study: The patchy adherence of CAs, the encouraging but error-prone ways in which domain name holders configure CAA records, and the handful DNS operators that even enable their customers to configure CAA records. Using historic CAA data, we uncover anomalies in issued certificates, and suggest several ways to improve the security impact of CAA. Dissemination of our results to the community has led to specific improvements at several CAs and revocation of mis-issued certificates. We commit to reproducible research and share raw data and analyses tools.
